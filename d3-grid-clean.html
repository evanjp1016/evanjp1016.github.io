January 2018 - April 2018

Redesigning a library of open data for Detroit, Michigan with Data Driven Detroit

In early 2018, I worked with Data Driven Detroit (D3) and a small student team to redesign D3’s Toolbox, an online library of over 1,500 community-centric data resources, to help their mission of combatting uninformed decision-making towards urban development and community projects around Detroit, Michigan.

Overview

Context

Client project and UndergraduateBachelors program at the University of Michigan School of Information capstone project

Team

Four UX Design students, two D3 team members

Contribution

UX research, UX design, visual design

Problem

User feedback has indicated that poor structure and a lack of helpful information about the open-data resourcesTools, maps, and other collections of data that offer statistical and numerical insights on a variety of different subjects in D3's Toolbox has hindered peoples’ ability to find useful data resources and diminished their desire to make data-driven decisions. The results can negatively affect Detroit Communities as people proceed with development and projects that may unknowingly work in opposition to how the surrounding communities actually function socially, economically, or spatially.

Goal

My team’s goal was to redesign D3’s Toolbox for simplicity and scalability. Our redesign was intended to help people quickly search through the long list of data resources and identify resources useful to their projects as well as preserve the Toolbox's ease of use the as it grows with more open-data resources.

My role

While my teammates and I shared the general roles of researchers and designers, my personal role with the team also included leading meetings, discussions, and presentations with our D3 and others who assisted us throughout the project.

Problems with D3's existing Toolboxes

To understand pain points in D3's existing Toolboxes, we conducted in-person usability tests with five people that fell into one of three categories: experienced in urban planning, experienced in data analysis, or invested in Detroit communities.

Three existing Toolboxes complicated where to find the Toolbox

D3 actually had three separate Toolboxes: their original Toolbox live on their website, a prototyped Toolbox built prior to our involvement, and the Open Data Portal on an affiliated website. The inability to direct people to a single, all-inclusive Toolbox was the first step in a complicated journey of finding useful data resources.

A lack of descriptive information clouded the usefulness of each resource

People were not provided enough descriptive information about each available resource to know if it would be useful to them. Despite having information on the different topics, time-spans, and locations that each data resource covered and the mediums through which they were accessible, D3 only included a portion of it in each Toolbox.

Unintentionally camouflaged filters were mistaken for menus or navigation

Search and filter elements on D3’s original and prototyped toolboxes were rarely used to find resources as they were unintentionally camouflaged on the webpage, often resembling menus or navigation. The Open Data Portal, on the other hand, forced people to filter by category, then gave limited filters beyond that point.

Solutions in the redesign

D3 had one IT specialist who would implement our changes into their Toolbox, all three of which were built with Wordpress. In addition to creating a single, all-inclusive Toolbox, it was important that we limited our changes in functionality and placed more focus on restructuring the Toolbox elements that already existed.

A hierarchy of descriptive information to identify usefulness of each resource

To address the lack of descriptive information for each resource, discussion with from usability testers and D3 helped us specify 10 pieces of descriptive information that, if applicable, should be displayed for each resource:

LISTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTTT

A list of horizontal, rectangular cards offered ample space for our 10 pieces of descriptive information. To promote easy skimming through long lists of resources, we developed a hierarchy of information on each card, from visual identification elements to broad text elements to detailed text elements. Informal usability testing with low-fidelity prototypes confirmed that our new card-based resource list helped people more easily decide whether or not a resource was useful for their needs.

More apparent filtering similar to popular e-commerce websites

Next we explored how to address the problem of unintentionally camouflaged filters. Our best performing design in informal usability tests pulled inspiration from conventional online retail interfaces. We placed two rows of large resource-category buttons above the resource list to make this a more visible filtering step before browsing resources, then placed more granular filters (resource-type, location, timeframe) in a panel on the left side of the screen.

Final Product

Prototype

Our final design incorporated our design solutions along with revamped visual design. D3 planned to incorporate primarily structural design changes into their product as opposed to visual design changes, though we felt that to reduce bias in our final usability testing, we had to present our prototype as closely to a production website as possible.

Outcome

After outperforming D3's three existing Toolboxes in usability testing, our design inspired some of the changes seen in D3's largest Toolbox, the Open Data Portal.

Our prototype outperformed D3's three existing toolboxes in terms of completion time and perceived difficulty

With 8 new usability testers, we tested our design against D3's three existing Toolboxes with numerous usability tasks. Our final prototype outperformed D3’s three existing Toolboxes in terms of completion time and perceived difficulty (based on a Likert scale from 1 - very easy to 5 - very difficult).

Changes implemented into D3's Open Data Portal

While our redesign has not seen much of an impact on the Toolbox available on D3’s live website, many of our structural design changes seem to have impacted the design of the current (early 2020) resource listings in the Open Data Portal.
