Redesigning a library of open data for Detroit, Michigan with Data Driven Detroit

In early 2018, I worked with Data Driven Detroit (D3) and a small student team to redesign D3’s Toolbox, an online library of over 1,500 community-centric data resourcesTools, maps, and other collections of data that offer statistical and numerical insights on a variety of different subjects, to help their mission of combatting uninformed decision-making towards urban development and community projects around Detroit, Michigan.

Context

Client project and UMSIUniversity of Michigan School of Information capstone project

Team

Four UX Design students, two D3 team members

Contribution

UX research, UX design, visual design

Overview

Problem

User feedback has indicated that poor structure and a lack of helpful information about the open-data resourcesTools, maps, and other collections of data that offer statistical and numerical insights on a variety of different subjects in D3's Toolbox has hindered peoples’ ability to find useful data resources and diminished their desire to make data-driven decisions. The results can negatively affect Detroit Communities as people proceed with development and projects that may unknowingly work in opposition to how the surrounding communities actually function socially, economically, or spatially.

Goal

My team’s goal was to redesign D3’s Toolbox for simplicity and scalability. Our redesign was intended to help people quickly search through the long list of data resources and identify resources useful to their projects as well as preserve the Toolbox's ease of use the as it grows with more open-data resources.

My Role

While my teammates and I shared the general roles of researchers and designers, my personal role with the team also included leading meetings, discussions, and presentations with our D3 and others who assisted us throughout the project.

Problems with D3's existing Toolboxes

D3 actually had three existing Toolboxes, which complicated the first step of finding the Toolbox.

The first step to making data-informed decisions was to find the Toolbox. Between their Original Toolbox, a new prototyped Toolbox, and the affiliated Open Data Portal, people didn't know which Toolbox to use.

Testing revealed that a lack of descriptive information clouded the usefulness of each resource listed in the Toolboxes.

The next step to help people make informed decisions was to help them easily find useful data resources within the Toolbox. However, people did not know enough about each available resource to know whether or not it was useful to them. To be able to skim long lists and easily identify useful data resources, people would need broad descriptors like a title, a topic, and a short description as well as more detailed descriptors like their corresponding time-spans, locations, and the mediums through which they are available. While D3 had this information stored, the Toolboxes only displayed a portion of it.

Search and filter elements were unintentionally camouflaged on the webpage.

Already struggling to find useful data resources, people rarely used the available search and filter elements to narrow their search. Tester feedback noted that filters on D3’s original and prototyped Toolboxes were mistaken for menus or navigation. The Open Data Portal, in a slightly different manner, forced people to filter by category, but then gave limited filters beyond that point.

Solutions in the redesign

We designed a hierarchy of descriptive information to help identify usefulness of each resource in order to address their lack of descriptive information.

The first step of this process was to specify 10 pieces of descriptive information that should be displayed for each resource and fit these into a confined space for individual resource listings.

The next step of this process was to develop a hierarchy of information on each card that would promote easy skimming through long lists of resources. This drew the viewers eyes from visual identification elements to broad text elements to detailed text elements.

We designed more apparent filtering similar to popular e-commerce websites to address the problem of unintentionally camouflaged filters.

In addition to helping people more easily find useful data resources, visible and helpful filtering ensures that the Toolbox remains usable as the number of resources continued to grow. Early exploration with poor testing results pushed us to look at strong conventions for digital filters that have proven their success; in this case, online retail interfaces.

Our best performing design in informal usability tests placed two rows of large resource-category buttons above the resource list to make this a more visible filtering step before browsing resources, then placed more granular filters (resource-type, location, timeframe) in a panel on the left side of the screen.

While we identified and addressed more problems that just these, for brevity I'll just be covering these in this study.

Final Product and Outcome

Our final design and prototype incorporated our design solutions along with revamped visual design.

D3 planned to incorporate primarily structural design changes into their product as opposed to visual design changes, though we felt that to reduce bias in our final usability testing, we had to present our prototype as closely to a production website as possible.

After outperformed D3's three existing Toolboxes, some of our design suggestions were implemented into D3's Open Data Portal.

With 8 new usability testers, we tested our design against D3's three existing Toolboxes with numerous usability tasks. Both Average Task Completion Time and Percieved Difficulty (based on a Likert scale) were lower for our prototype than each of D3s existing Toolboxes.

While you won't see a replica of our design on any of D3's websites, many of our structural design changes seem to have impacted the design of the current (2020) resource listings in the Open Data Portal.
