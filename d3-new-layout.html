January 2018 - April 2018

Redesigning a library of open data for Detroit, Michigan with Data Driven Detroit

Data Driven Detroit (D3) is a data hub for Detroit, Michigan and its surrounding communities. They collect, analyze, interpret, and share accurate and unbiased, community-centric dataData regarding education, neighborhoods, businesses, demographics, etc. to combat uninformed decision-making towards development and projects in the area.

Overview

Context

Client project and UMSIUniversity of Michigan School of Information capstone project

Duties

UX research, UX design, visual design

Team

Four UX Design students, two D3 team members

Problem

D3 offers over 1,500 open-data resourcestools, maps, and other collections of data that offer statistical and numerical insights on a variety of different subjects about the Detroit area in a digital library known as their “Toolbox”. However, inadequate organization and presentation of these resources hinders peoples’ ability and desire to find and use data resources, resulting in uninformed decision making towards projects that can negatively affect Detroit communities.

Goal

My team’s goal was to redesign D3’s Toolbox to introduce an organizational system for data resources that is simple and scalable, helping people quickly and easily find resources useful to them and remaining easy to use as the Toolbox grows.

My role

While my teammates and I shared the general roles of UX researchers and UX designers, my personal role with on the team also included leading the outward communication of our project. I often lead meetings, discussions, and presentations with our client, UMSIUniversity of Michigan School of Information faculty members, and others who assisted us throughout the project.

Starting with research

Three existing Toolbox designs

The first month of this project entailed researching pain points within D3’s existing Toolbox. As it turned out, D3 actually had three separate versions of the Toolbox:

  1. The original Toolbox live on their website
  2. A prototyped Toolbox, built by D3 prior to our involvement
  3. A Toolbox on an affiliated website known as the Open Data Portal

Caption

Defining our audience and testing

Starting with heuristic evaluation and competitive analysis, we later shifted our research to focus on interviews and usability testing. We defined our target audience as people from three groups:

  1. People with data analysis experience
  2. Detroit community members without data analysis experience
  3. Urban Planners

What we found is outlined below:

Problems with the existing Toolboxes

Not enough descriptive information for each resource

While using the Toolboxes, 5 of 5 usability testers felt that they didn’t know enough about each available resource to know whether or not it would be useful to them. This was due to a lack of descriptive information about each resource.

Descriptive information for a resource includes basic information like a title and a short description as well as more granular information like what type of resource it is (map, dataset, presentation, etc.), when it’s data was collected, and what locations it addresses. Despite having this information for most resources, D3 only included a portion of it in each Toolbox.

Caption

Unintentionally camouflaged filters

While D3’s existing Toolbox designs did include search boxes and/or filters, 3 of 5 usability testers did not use or notice available search and filter elements. Instead, these testers read each resource’s title or description in order to determine what resources would be useful to them.

We found that in D3’s original and prototyped Toolbox these existing search and filter elements were unintentionally camouflaged on the webpage, often resembling menus or navigation, while the Open Data Portal forced people to filter by category, then gave limited filters beyond that point.

Caption

Solutions in the redesign

Building a hierarchy of descriptive information

As noted above, a lack of descriptive information for each resource slowed peoples’ ability to find useful resources. Based on feedback from usability testers and discussion with D3, we specified 10 pieces of desired descriptive information that should be displayed for each resource:

  • Title
  • Description
  • Category
  • Resource Type
  • Thumbnail Image
  • Date/timeframe
  • Popularity Badge (if applicable)
  • Download button (if applicable)
  • Metadata (location, filetype, file size)
  • Project partners (if applicable)

To design a skimmable way to convey all of this information in a small space, early ideas took inspiration from job-posting websites by creating a card-based layout for the Toolboxes’ resources.

Caption

We found that a list of horizontal, rectangular cards made for easy skimming and ample space for our 10 pieces of descriptive information without requiring too much real estate on the webpage. For the content of each card, we developed a hierarchy of this descriptive information to guide peoples’ eyes from visual identification elements to broad text elements to detailed text elements.

Caption

Caption

Informal usability testing with low-fidelity prototypes confirmed that our new card-based resource design offered more informative skimming than D3’s existing Toolbox designs.

Filtering similar to e-commerce

As we discovered, unintentionally camouflaged filters slowed peoples’ ability to find useful resources. Early iterations to address this aimed to allow broad filtering via checkboxes for each resource-category on the left side of the screen (kids & education, housing & neighborhood, demographics, etc.), while more granular filters (resource-type, location, timeframe) lived at the top of the resource listing.

Caption

These early iterations did not perform well in informal usability testing. Again, people did not use the filters, often noting that they mistook them for some type of navigation menu. To remedy this, later iterations of filter elements pulled inspiration from conventional online retail interfaces.

Caption

We placed more granular filters (resource-type, location, timeframe) in a panel on the left side of the screen and reshaped our broad resource-category checkboxes as large buttons, placing them above the resource list with multiple rows to make this a more obvious filtering step before browsing the full list of resources.

Caption

Caption

A second round of informal usability testing with low-fidelity prototypes confirmed that our updated filter layout offered more noticeable filters that better resembled what people were used to seeing from other websites.

Evaluation and final deliverables

Formally testing our design

A prototype with our design changes was tested with 8 new usability testers against D3’s three existing Toolboxes. Specific tasks were given based on whether the testers were experienced with data analysis, inexperienced with data analysis, or experienced in urban planning. We recorded completion time and perceived difficulty (based on a Likert scale from 1 - very easy to 5 - very difficult) as well as gathered any qualitative feedback for each task.

Caption

While our prototype had lower average completion times and lower average difficulty for each task, 2 of the 8 testers noted that they preferred the visual design of D3's prototyped Toolbox over ours. Knowing this, D3 did not see it necessary for us to revise our visual design choices as they planned to incorporate primarily structural design changes into their product.

Final deliverables

Our final deliverables included high fidelity mockups, a functional prototype, and a written report that described our research findings and design solutions.

Reflection

What I would have done differently

The weakest point of this project was our usability testing. We formally tested numerous designs with only 5 people in our early research, between 5 and 10 more people informally throughout the design phase, and 8 new people during our final evaluation of our design. While all of these numbers are fairly low, the big issue was in the potential for bias in these tests.

While we tested with people that roughly fell into our target audience, the people we tested were chosen out of convenience. They were all either students or faculty at our university, or friends and family. They may have offered decent insight into pain points with the interface we were testing, but they did not accurately represent the people that would end up using this toolbox in the end. This “convenience testing” may have just as well introduced bias as a result of age, technological experience, accessibility needs, and familiarity with the Detroit area.

While we had discussed traveling to Detroit (only ~40 miles away) to test with actual people from the area that fell into our target audience, time constraints and a lack of preparation obstructed this goal. Looking back, this is something we should have allocated much more effort into achieving in order to be sure that our testing was revealing issues that our true audience would identify.
